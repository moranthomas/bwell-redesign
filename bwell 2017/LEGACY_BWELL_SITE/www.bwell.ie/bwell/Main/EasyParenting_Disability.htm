About Allison

Allison Keating is a registered pyschologist of the Psychological Society of Ireland. She has a Masters In Psychology and a Degree in Behavioural Science in Psychology. She is a regular pscyhological media contributor on TV, radio , papers and magazines. The bWell Clinic is a progressive, emphatic psychology clinic that uses CBT - solution focused therapy, pscyo-dynamic therapy and positive psychology for pscyhological well being specialising in anxiety, panic attack, relationships and advocating good mental health. Click here to follow the bWell Clinic on facebook for relevent and interesting topics on all things to do with your mind and emotions.
      
Q: I’m in my early thirties, and have been with my partner for the past five years. Last year we decided to start a family, and after some disappointments, I finally conceived. However, four months into the pregnancy we were told the baby is at high risk of having a disability. At first my partner was really supportive and positive, but his enthusiasm gradually started waning, and recently he has hinted that he can’t cope anymore. We used to have a brilliant relationship, but argue constantly now. I can’t force him to stay, but how can I go through this alone?
      
      
A: I am so sorry to hear of the difficult time that you are going through. Pregnancy alone is an emotionally charged roller-coaster and this must be so worrying for you. It must have been so difficult to hear the news that your baby may have a possible disability. I don't know if you have had any more specialist information or further testing and if you are awaiting more results that must be excruciating.

Hearing this news must be so difficult fraught with a mixture of emotions ranging from the initial sense of shock, denial, fear and disbelief. Thoughts such as 'why me' or 'why us' can lead into a rising sense of frustration, anger and feelings of guilt especially if one partner is feeling like they can't cope with it. Added to this you have to contend with your partners fears which are to be expected but there seems to be fear that he may leave if he can't cope with this. Could you both talk with a professional who could help mediate you through the very difficult questions that need to be raised and answered with the knowledge that you have at present. He has hinted that he can't cope anymore. Perhaps you could ask him to speak about his feelings with a mental health practitioner on his own and I would encourage the same for yourself.

This honesty is likely to create more emotional upset but it is a necessary conversation to know what to do next. I imagine that you might be feeling a great sense of helplessness, uncertainty and fear.

I would suggest that you need to talk to each other really honestly. This will be extremely difficult but it is very important in understanding what you both think and feel and what you both hope or are fearful of in the future. This will determine if you can get through this together or if you need to potentially split up?

It is wonderful to have a supportive partner especially when you are pregnant because it can be a time whereby you can feel more vulnerable. But, with respect to partners and husbands out there, it is you that carries the baby, and ultimately it will be you that will bring your child into this world. This is a very emotionally charged situation for you, you are pregnant, you have been told of a possibility of a disability and now your partner with whom you had a great relationship is feeling as if he is not able to deal with this. There must be a huge sense of loss and sadness and I think it is important to validate these feelings for yourself. Even to validate the feelings of sadness that it was hard to conceive and now your pregnancy has gone from being a happy event to one of being full of unknowns and uncertainty about your relationships future and your babies health. My full empathy and compassion as a clinician and mother are with you.

Have you been put in touch with other parents who are expecting and are in a similar situation? Are there any specific support groups that could be of help to you? These are possible questions you could ask your paediatrician. I would suggest writing out any questions, fears, and worries for each ante-natal visit to try and help understand the diagnosis and what this potentially means for the next 6 months, when the child is born and for their future development. Does the hospital have a disability advisor? You may have done all of this, so apologies in advance if you already have.

Becoming a parent brings with it a catalyst of emotional changes. All the clichés are true and your life will never be the same. You didn't mention how you feel about the news of the possibility of a disability? I wonder how you are feeling? Are you getting any support from family and friends? I would suggest that you need to have a buffer of emotional and physical support from family and friends. You also need to engage in consistent self-care, whether it is a relaxing Mp3 at the end of the day in bed or a relaxing bath or walk. E-mail me at allison@bwell.ie and I will send you on a pre-labour relaxation Mp3 to help with the labour experience.
      
      
      
      
I suppose this is where I would like to return to, to you and the baby. In terms of becoming a parent you can experience depths of unconditional love that you might never have experienced before and it can also be peppered with sacrifice and copious amounts of selflessness. Without knowing you or your partner I am fully aware that I am only gleaning a snapshot of this issue. Having said that, being part of a partnership there is a commitment to each other, and even though you are not married I would imagine that it would be only normal having been together five years that you have experienced ups and down as would only be normal. These normal joys and disappointments are part of the human condition, as is uncertainty and being out of control.

Mother nature is very much still in charge in the realm of pregnancy even amidst all of our technological and medical advancements the miracle of growing a child resonates deep within your belly. What I mean by this is in pregnancy you have to go with it, and I think this can throw a spanner in the works for us modern women as we cannot predict nor control the outcome. This can be unnerving at the best of times but faced with the possible risks of a disability it would be enough to test the strongest of relationships. Maybe give yourselves permission to experience the emotions that are coming to the fore. This may enable you to move towards what to do next. Even to decide to just try and support each other. If this is not possible then that harsh reality will need to be faced. But I would encourage you to really look after your physical, emotional and mental needs at the moment as it is more challenging for you as the carrying mother.

A really helpful CBT technique is called 'soften, soothe, allow', here is the link

https://www.box.com/files/1/f/468568472/1/f_3986098514

it will help you acknowledge and name your feelings and emotions, validate them and allow yourself to move through them mindfully.

It's interesting because as a very practical CBT Psychologist I am always the one showing the client how to take control of what life presents to them from within because you can't be in control of your external environment. In the case of pregnancy though, the role is reversed and you have to be patient and to literally wait and see. In terms of the possible disability is it definitive? I suppose this in terms of your relationship isn't the pivotal point because we are talking about your child here and I suppose the basic rhetoric of parenting is supposed to be based on unconditional love. Your child has not yet been born, and he is backing away, and this is not meant to sound judgemental but he needs to be there for you and your baby and if he can't it is not a good sign for things to come. But perhaps if you have a very mediated supportive and explorative conversation it might provide a framework to help with what to do next.

You are so right you cannot force him to stay and unfortunately you are going to have to let him decide what he wants to do. Even though this will be extremely hard in your present situation. I sincerely wish you luck, now in your pregnancy and when your child arrives. Look after yourself and have compassion for the stress that you are currently within. Allison x
