About Allison

Allison Keating is a registered pyschologist of the Psychological Society of Ireland. She has a Masters In Psychology and a Degree in Behavioural Science in Psychology. She is a regular pscyhological media contributor on TV, radio , papers and magazines. The bWell Clinic is a progressive, emphatic psychology clinic that uses CBT - solution focused therapy, pscyo-dynamic therapy and positive psychology for pscyhological well being specialising in anxiety, panic attack, relationships and advocating good mental health. Click here to follow the bWell Clinic on facebook for relevent and interesting topics on all things to do with your mind and emotions.
      
Q: My husband is a football fanatic, and each championship and football league I'm told is more important than the last. I am really fed up though and feel dumped when football season is on. It's the amount of time and all the other activities that go with it. If he watches it at home, we're watching TV in two separate rooms and not talking. It's as if I don't exist, or if he goes out with the lads he drinks way too much and then he is no good to me or the kids the next day. Am I being overly sensitive here?
      
      
When the colour of the TV screen is predominantly football green you can start to get green with envy. Like most issues in relationships they can sound trivial when you say it to your partner, but the feelings you express are much deeper than the presenting issue. It is the circumstantial behaviour that needs to be understood and your emotions and feelings need to be heard and acknowledged. You say that you feel 'dumped' and it seems that you feel replaced by the football. What emotion are you feeling here? Do you feel sad, lonely and or hurt? You need to sit down and understand how all this makes you feel, do this in writing and in private so that you can explain your feelings much more logically to your partner without it turning into what he probably sees as a nagging session. When you can clearly say how his behaviour makes you feel he can take in the emotions you are talking about rationally and then he can interpret it and work towards a mutually satisfying goal, sorry, excuse the pun!

Apart from football season do you feel valued and loved in your relationship? There a sense of you feeling abandoned, and lonely. It is healthy to have separate time to pursue your own unique hobbies or interests. But time is so scarce for couples and this seems to spill-over into the next day if he is hung-over. Do you feel resentful? Do you feel the family work load is equal? Do you have time for yourself? The answers to these questions will help you identify the specifics of how you feel. These are practical based problems that you can resolve together which might result in scheduling time for your own pursuits to balance any possible deficit.

It's the emotional score that I am more interested in. What is really troubling you? Do you feel emotionally supported at other times or does football season highlight genuine sore or raw issues in your relationship. I have noticed over the years that women in families can sometimes lose themselves as a result of being a mother, with your own needs taking a major backseat. Whereas men are actually better at recognizing the importance of time on things outside the family for themselves. I advise female clients in this position to play the boys at their own game and I mean this with total sincerity. The men are right, you need to carve time for yourself, otherwise resentment and anger become familiar friends that will take up unwelcome residence in your home. Before you even think it, I am going to inoculate against the angry 'but I don't have enough time as it is already'. Yes, you are right we live in an unbalanced world where work often comes before play and rest is a distant figment of your fettered imagination. This is where the very unspontaneous but brilliant diary solution comes into action.
      
      
      
      
      
Sit down and write out the week or month ahead and put in the activities that you want to do together (including the very American but essential date night), family activities and personal time. Within football season this will let your husband have his football time given with a generous spirit. I don't believe in comprise because someone also losses out. So, give the time to him with duchenne smile, which in English is real and genuine smile, you know when the crows feet come out and match the upturned 'u' on your face.

Limit the big nights out to Finals, and put some ground rules down for less important matches so that he won't incapacitated the next day. By writing it down together it will help put boundaries around the situation for you. Now, add, some time for yourself and your interests into this mix. Put down times for yourself, and stick to it. Value time off for you as well, this is where I feel women get it wrong sometimes. It is so important to value yourself as an individual and it is very important for your relationship as well. You will come back feeling replenished and bit more like yourself. A lot of women feel to do this is to be selfish. But, I would have to argue that to live in a house with two arguing, cross and resentful adults is not a good environment for children. So you owe it to yourself, your husband and yourself to do like the men do and take the time that you deserve. He is not being selfish and neither are you, once you have set down the boundaries that you are both happy with. Maybe football season may be the start of a new equalizer in your relationship!
