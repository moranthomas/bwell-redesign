About Allison

Allison Keating is a registered pyschologist of the Psychological Society of Ireland. She has a Masters In Psychology and a Degree in Behavioural Science in Psychology. She is a regular pscyhological media contributor on TV, radio , papers and magazines. The bWell Clinic is a progressive, emphatic psychology clinic that uses CBT - solution focused therapy, pscyo-dynamic therapy and positive psychology for pscyhological well being specialising in anxiety, panic attack, relationships and advocating good mental health. Click here to follow the bWell Clinic on facebook for relevent and interesting topics on all things to do with your mind and emotions.
      
Q; My husband had a stroke six months ago and it has had a profound impact on our relationship. Firstly, I think he has lost a lot of his confidence, our sex life has changed and I feel somewhat rejected by this. He is doing all the things his Doctor has advised him but I see such a change in him and I feel quite lonely as I don't want to upset him or do anything that could cause another stroke. He finds it difficult to say how he feels and this worries me as I find it hard to know when he is upset.
G, 59
      
      
Hi G, thank you for your e-mail, I am sorry to hear about your husbands stroke and how it has had a ripple effect not just for him but for you both emotionally in your relationship. Cardiac Health is an area we in Ireland need to really understand in terms of the emotional impact being as much a player as the physical aspect. It seems obvious that the heart is the place that is most synonymous with feelings, of love, excitement, sadness, anxiety and grief. But research has shown relationships between anger and hostility in men and anxiety in women as having a major influence in CHD (cardiac health disease). As a Psychologist, otherwise known as the 'physicians of the soul' and in clinical sense we recognize your emotional health is where 'the heart of the matter' can lie.

For years it was considered that the Type-A personality was more connected to heart attacks, the ambitious, irritable ,hurried type, but there is the less known Type-D personality that may link in with what you have said about your husband. The Type -D personality is quiet, finds expressing their emotions difficult and would need their head to be hanging off before a trip to the Doctor was considered necessary. What we need to think about from your husbands heart health is twofold, what would be of benefit to him and what you want to improve within your relationship.

Perhaps, go out to dinner, or go for walk, somewhere neutral but where a conversation can be had. The idea is to nurture a conversation not kill it with accusations, or starting with statements like 'you never tell me how you feel.' There is an art to conversation and this art can dry up in couples sometimes because they know each other too well, and the consequences of honesty can be very scary and harrowing. What would it mean to tell my husband how I feel? What if he gives me an answer I don't like? These are consequences couples sometimes are afraid to face. If your husband is to open up to you it needs to feel safe, trustworthy and most importantly that what he says is respected. His feelings need to be validated by you actively listening to what he is saying. This is easier said than done, because couples know each other so well, this is the problem. If a stranger was to open up to you, you wouldn't jump in and make assumptions about them or tell them how they feel. This is where the art of genuinely listening, which is not natural, needs to come into action. So, if you want your husband to talk to you, then listen. Listen without prejudice, listen without interrupting, listen with empathy and listen with your heart.

In terms of your sex life, this is very common in men after cardiac health disease with a reported 50% of men saying the never have sex. There could be a physical component to that in terms of blood flow, fear of another stroke, a lower libido because of feeling depressed after a cardiac incident coupled with a major drop in confidence. This can be tackled by talking with your GP or Cardiac consultant, and then together as a couple. Talk openly and honestly about how you feel specifically that you feel rejected and that you are feeling lonely within the relationship. The change in your sex life may have nothing to do with you, so it is important to understand what is going on for your husband and how to change this empathically as a couple.
      
      
      
      

The HeartMath Institute talk about a little known fact that heart actually sends more signals to the brain than the brain sends to the heart! These heart signals have a very significant effect on brain function with a specific influence on emotional processing. I recently attended a relationship conference where Dr. Gottman a world-renowned marriage and relationship Psychologist spoke about how our heart and mind affect our ability to communicate as part of a couple. In his research setting dubbed 'The Love Lab' he monitored couples having arguments whilst being monitored, he found that once the heart rate went over 100 beats per minute that it affected the person's ability to think. It was like the hearts response to emotions or words with contempt, criticism and or lack of respect had a direct heart and mind impact. Interesting in the case of men who find it difficult to express their emotions maybe their brain's response to the heart has a direct impact on their ability to articulate how they feel. Gottman's advice - that every couple should have a finger monitor to show where you heart rate is at! I found this fascinating as he said the couples who were been videotaped (this was old style research!) didn't visibly look to distressed but their internal biology was telling a very different story. In terms of how to fix this problem, he said to walk away from an argument that wasn't going the way you felt you wanted it to go. Tell your partner that you need twenty minutes, and that when the couples did this and their physiology had calmed down he said it was like having a brain transplant and the person could then say what they needed to say. I thought this was such a helpful tactic because not many couples are monitoring the heart and mind's reaction but they may become more and more stuck in never been able to express how they genuinely feel. It is contrary to a lot of relationship tips of just sticking out the argument, but I have to agree that if you feel you need that time out, that it could be a life saver.

On top of working on this together as a couple I would suggest cardiac counselling to deal with emotions such as hostility and anger and possibly depression. Cardiac counselling will give your husband the chance to discuss how he feels about the stroke, what it meant to him, and how he feels about the future. To learn how to be able to say how he feels within your relationship and to have his emotional needs met. Giving him time to reflect on issues or circumstances that may have contributed to this not just from a physical perspective but from an emotional one. To rebuild his confidence and to create a new sense of purpose, hope and meaning within his life. With the intention of creating a renewed shared meaning as a couple and to place priority right back at the heart of the relationship. Best of luck to you both. Allison x
