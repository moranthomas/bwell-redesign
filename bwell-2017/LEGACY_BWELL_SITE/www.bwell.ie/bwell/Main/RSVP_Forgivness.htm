About Allison

Q: My wife and I are together a very long time. We have a pretty good marriage but there is one area that I really fall down in. I find I bear grudges and hold onto anger and resentment for issues that are well past at this stage and that I know I should have already gotten over. Most of the time it is fine but when we argue I can go from 0 to 100 in what seems like seconds. I have ruined important nights out, and other important occasions. I just wish I could keep my mouth shut and let it go, but it is a very strong reaction and I can be seethe for days afterwards. Do you have any advice to let it go? J
      
      
The emotions that you are talking about are very emotionally and physically heated and raw. It is a common misconception that time is a healer. Time certainly helps put some distance between issues and yourself but you have to take responsibility for your emotions if they haven't healed. To let past emotions heal you need to firstly know what they are, and secondly do something about them. It's interesting, I frequently hear nearly shame or embarrassment from clients when they say they haven't dealt with something sooner. But, the truth is you need to make the decision to change and then you have to act on those thought intentions. Thinking alone won't change this, nor will ruminating over past hurts. It takes a lot of courage to take this first step as well.

So, first things first, you sound really hurt. Do you know what you are hurt about? You may think that you do because of certain specific instances but, the big question is, do you really, insightfully understand what those feelings meant to you. What I mean by
this is that like a physical wound we can also be wounded emotionally. These emotional wounds can leave scars that no tissue can absorb. Did the incident(s) in question bring up other emotional wounds from even before your marriage? Sometimes the issues that hit the nerve are the ones that have been trampled on before and by that nature they are very sensitive. You need to write out the answers to these questions. Because you when you speak with your wife you need to know what it is that you felt in order to start the forgiveness process.

Anger and resentment is specifically bad to men’s heart health. It literally hardens the cardiac walls. This hostility coupled with anger has been correlated in many studies to being a contributing factor in being susceptible to cardiac health disorders. When people hold onto grudges it is damaging on some many levels. Obviously you feel bad, it is not a joyous feeling. You may feel an injustice has occurred to you that was unfair, and you may be right.
      
      
      
      
      
I have asked many clients to forgive unspeakable acts that have happened to them. In reality they have every right to feel angry, wronged, upset often coupled with a great sense of loss perhaps of the quality of their lives as a result of a bad or bad experiences. I do not know the specifics of what happened between your wife and yourself but ultimately the event is less important than how you reacted to it. We are seldom given the correct relationship tools to deal with high emotionally provoking situations. Out of the bag of emotions anger is very seductive as our reptilian brain becomes defensive, and feels it needs to fight the instigator of our annoyance. Anger is a very physical feeling and it is easy to lose control over it. This is why you need to address what it is that you are angry about, what it meant to you and to take the next step to allowing yourself release these pent up grudges. They are so toxic. By forgiving your wife you are freeing yourself. You are allowing yourself be part of humanity which is to allow others especially those to whom we love to make mistakes, because we all make mistakes. This openness in relationships for a bit of room to breathe and be human will help the longevity of your relationship.

Anger, hostility and grudges are like carrying a very large weight in your heart. It can be easy to sit back and say that you are justified in being and staying angry. But, what will that get you? These are tough answers for you, but the person who losses out is you.

I have always thought that the term 'the art of forgiveness' is especially spot on.
