About Allison

Allison Keating is a registered pyschologist of the Psychological Society of Ireland. She has a Masters In Psychology and a Degree in Behavioural Science in Psychology. She is a regular pscyhological media contributor on TV, radio , papers and magazines. The bWell Clinic is a progressive, emphatic psychology clinic that uses CBT - solution focused therapy, pscyo-dynamic therapy and positive psychology for pscyhological well being specialising in anxiety, panic attack, relationships and advocating good mental health. Click here to follow the bWell Clinic on facebook for relevent and interesting topics on all things to do with your mind and emotions.
      
Q: I am a 38 year old woman and I ended a seven year relationship last year. My partner had become emotionally abusive and it really took its toll on my emotional health. Anyhow, it is taking time for me to move on and recover and I seem to have lost a lot of confidence. I used to love meeting up with friends and going to parties but recently I have started to feel queasy and sick before heading out and I start to feel really anxious. It's affecting my social life and my work, as a lot of my job involves meeting new clients etc. What can I do to make myself feel better and get back to my normal self?
      
      
A: Thank you for you letter, I hope this response helps you and many more, because emotional wounds cut so deep. Like any issues with mental health a large proportion of the issue is it's invisibility, if you wore a cast, or were outwardly injured it is obvious to people that you are hurt and need support. But emotional abuse is so divisive and hidden that the wounds that are physically hidden leave scar tissue that many a tissue will absorb.

The damaging effects of emotional abuse are exactly as you have described, you are robbed of self-confidence, which leaves people bereft. The reason for this is that self-confidence is a feeling. It is a feeling of being comfortable, when you are comfortable in your own skin, you have a internal platform that allows you to experience life from a safe and secure point of view. I can hear fear in your letter, now, self-doubt replaces that secure knowledge of who you are. This fear can be crippling especially in social or work aspects as you mentioned above. Coming out of a relationship is hard, coming out of an emotionally abusive relationship requires some therapeutic work on your behalf. I would recommend going on your personal therapeutic journey with a mental health practitioner. You need to gain insight into how you got into this relationship. I understand how you stayed because these relationships are so emotionally corrosive, from a professional point of view I hear the anguish from women and men who have endured, mostly for too long at the peril of someone else. So here are some questions that you should look at; What type of relationship did your parents have? Were there healthy boundaries? How did they communicate with each other? Was it safe to express their emotions? What did you learn about relationships from your home?

Dr. Clarissa Pinkola Estẻs wrote 'Women who run with the Wolves' a beautiful book filled with stories, intercultural myths to help women connect with their instinctive female psyche. The book has been described as 'Vitamins for the soul' and I couldn't think of a better starting point for you. Women are so multilayered and deep, we are walking oxymorons, we are gentle yet fierce (and not in a Tyra Banks depiction!). We are the nurturers of relationships, this is how your brain has been hardwired, to connect fully and wholly with other human beings. If we take a quick detour from your issue to illicit my point think of the phenomenon that is the book 'Fifty shades of grey' people are churning out mediocre social explanations for it's success but ultimately it comes down to one thing, in terms of turning a woman on, she is in love with the idea of commitment, love and romance. If we think of men, in terms of external forms of sexual excitement it is much more physical, not as soulful as a woman. This 'erotica' industry understands that women love the idea of being in love and connecting in the most sacred way through a relationship. So it is so devastating when not only does the relationship end, but it has impacted your heart that normal functioning pales into the background. Hope, excitement future plans palour. It has been shown that people can die of a broken heart. I wonder if any research has been done on the impact on a woman's brain and heart when she has experienced years of emotional abuse?

So, find yourself, maybe this might be the first time. Start very small, ask yourself, 'What do I like?' this could be as banal as do I want a coffee or a tea? You have to retrain your decision making process to be from your perspective because inevitably you have been tip toeing around your 'abuser' trying to please. Which by the way you would never have been able to do because he needs therapy to heal the wounds of his past. I sometimes wonder is this one of the reasons women stay in emotional abusive relationships, as if it is a reflection on them as a failure if they don't 'fix' this other person if they just give enough, or love enough. It will never be enough, but stop blaming yourself and start thinking and ploughing your resources internally.
      
      
      
      
      
We live in a society that is somewhat allergic to so-called negative emotions. You would not be doing yourself any favours by jumping out of how you feel too quickly. You need time to heal. It was seven years, that's nearly three times longer than most Hollywood marriages! I jest. But, seriously, you need to sit with your real emotions. They need to be brought up by a trained therapist, who can hold the outpouring of emotions such as anger (how could he do that to me), rage (how did I let him do this to me?), fear (I am alone now, how and what do I do next?), sadness (for the sense of loss of time, of yourself, and of the relationship because at some stage I'm imaging there was a good relationship). I think there is a grieving process when letting go of a long term relationship, and when it has been emotionally dysfunctional, it compounds into complex grief.

I wonder what support you have around you? Dip into this, even if some of these links are not as strong as they used to be, which often happens when all your energy is being poured into the relationship. Start small, connect with one friend you trust. Link back in with your family, draw in your support now like a secure comforting blanket. Take each day as it comes, do push yourself to engage in uncomfortable situations even if it is hard. If an social invite comes in, have a lovely shower, imagine the shackles releasing down the drain and breathe in a sense of choice. You can choose to stay at home, ruminating in the past our you can walk into the life that you wanted. You have left, therefore you did that for a reason, it would have taken huge courage to do that. You are strong, you are courageous, going out for a night with friends is a piece of cake for you! Start looking in a perverse way at all the strengths your dysfunctional relationship would have brought to you. You are probably a keen negotiater, you can read situations, people much quicker than others. Be careful with this one though because this is coming from you being on hyper alert all the time.

In normal, functioning and healthy relationships you do not need to be in a state of being hyper arousal all the time, this is why not just your emotional health but your physical health will have been impacted. So, you need to be mindful of the anxiety you are feeling, it is interesting, maybe the anxiety is becoming worse for you now once the offender has been removed. This is often the case, because you 'knew' how to act not to get on his bad side, you knew the triggers that would set him off. This is what I mean about recognizing your perverse strengths that have come from your relationship.

You need to learn how to express your emotions in a healthy way. You need to figure out and create healthy boundaries for you. Unfortunately when people don't go through therapy when coming out of an emotional abusive relationship, they can enter into another one, and or shut down to the point that they unconsciously decide I'm never getting hurt again but this will then cease the possibility of ever creating a healthy relationship. In Clarrissa's book through psychoanalytic cantadora (storytelling)she points out that instinctively women know at the very beginning that within every woman there lives a powerful force, filled with good instincts, passionate creativity, and ageless knowing. She is the Wild Woman, who represents the instinctual nature of women. But she is an endangered species. In Women Who Run With the Wolves, Dr. Estés unfolds rich intercultural myths, fairy tales, and stories, many from her own family, in order to help women reconnect with the fierce, healthy, visionary attributes of this instinctual nature. Through the stories and commentaries in this remarkable book, we retrieve, examine, love, and understand the Wild Woman and hold her against our deep psyches as one who is both magic and medicine. Dr. Estés has created a new lexicon for describing the female psyche. Fertile and life-giving, it is a psychology of women in the truest sense, a knowing of the soul.
