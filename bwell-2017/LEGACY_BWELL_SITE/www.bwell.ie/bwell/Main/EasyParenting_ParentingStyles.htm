About Allison

Allison Keating is a registered pyschologist of the Psychological Society of Ireland. She has a Masters In Psychology and a Degree in Behavioural Science in Psychology. She is a regular pscyhological media contributor on TV, radio , papers and magazines. Q: My husband and I have very different parenting styles. He is more of an authoritarian and I am much more relaxed. Now that our three kids are not babies anymore, it's become more and more challenging to try and sort out squabbles etc. My husband charges in like a bull whereas I take a softer approach and try to encourage the kids to talk it out. I don't believe in reprimanding children all of the time for every little thing, whereas my husband sometimes behaves like a sergeant major. Do you have any advice on how we can try to get our parenting style more in sync? It's starting to cause frequent arguments between us.
      
      
A: Thanks for this great question as I think it will resonate with many couples out there. This can really cause major issues in a couples life as your children are going to be in the picture for quite some time so I do think it is something that needs addressing. I remember covering this possibly for the first time with my husband to be at our pre-marriage course. These difficult conversations about how you want to parent your children are a must, because our parental belief systems are so deeply embedded. It requires each person to sit down and to do some serious psychodynamic reflection about what belief systems belong to them and which are conditioned and inherited from their parents. And you just thought there were two in your marriage! Well, this is not the truth when it comes to parenting the specter of our family past, creates unconscious expectations of what you both consider the ‘norm’ or the right way to do it.

For this conversation to begin you need to sit down with your husband and bring in your own parents hypothetically. The most fundamental aspect for this to be a cooperative and helpful exercise is for you both to put down your own defenses. This is tougher in practice than it is in theory. This is not a blame game, remember your parents are just people as well. It is not a fault finding mission; it is to try and rationally stand back from an emotive experience that you were involved in and to try and see if you can learn from your parents mistakes and strengths and decide collaboratively on what values you want to place upon your children through your conscious parenting styles. Teamwork, remember that word. The stronger the urge you have to point the finger at how he feels your mum was too soft and you may feel his mum was too in charge, put down the parenting blame batons and breathe! If you haven’t done any training in this give yourself a break. Be kind, considerate to each other and work together.

Good questions you could start with are:

1.How did your parents discipline?

2.How did your parents show you affection?

3.How did you parents express themselves? How would they express anger, sadness, joy, disapproval?

4.What expectations did you feel your parents had of you as a child, teenager or adult?

5.Did your parents work as a team or did each parent have their own style?

6.How would you class each of your own parent parenting style and then your own?

According to Psychologist Diana Baumrind there are four types of parenting styles, this could be helpful in identifying which style you may use:

A.Authoritarian - Parent is the boss, strict rules, with punishment resulting in not following the pre-set rules.

B.Authoritative – There are clear rules and boundaries but the parents are more democratic and responsive to listening to the child’s needs.

C.Permissive – Very low demands, little or no discipline or punishment.

D.Uninvolved – The parent will look after their basic needs but will not be involved in their child’s life with the possibility of not minding them emotionally.

The authoritative parenting style is correlated most with children developing better self-esteem, being able to self-regulate better and functioning more optimally. The issue is how big is the gap between your style and your husbands? Ask your husband why he believes this to be a good way of parenting the children? Ask him if he is aware of their changing emotional and developmental needs as they have become older?

These are really tough questions so watch your tone when you ask them. You may feel angry at your husband with these as they are highly emotive questions. Work through why you feel upset and why you feel the authoritarian style is not matching your emotionally responsive approach. Have either of you done any reading, as this can be helpful for a number of reasons, it is unemotional and it can be helpful pointing out someone else’s theory without getting your own head bitten off.
      
      
      
      
      
      
From a Psychological point of view being an emotionally responsive parent who can provide clear boundaries is an excellent starting point. Perhaps a parenting course done together could be time well spent on your own as a couple and as parents. Solution Talk provide many training courses and seminars specific to the changing dynamics having teenagers.

We are talking about deep unconscious motivations, belief systems and embedded behaviour. No school is more influential and persuasive that what we have seen in our home of origin. For all the parenting advice that is readily available, it is how you act as a parent that will be the determining factor of how your child will learn. If you can’t express how you feel how can you expect your child to learn how to do this? If you lose control when your child or teenager doesn’t agree with you, how can you expect them to learn how to problem solve, co-operate or be flexible. Children learn what they see.

From a positive perspective, even if you feel upset and angry, this is a wonderful opportunity to open up and with real honesty and integrity decide how you want to parent together. This does not mean you have to be the exact same, but you need to dust off old belief systems that may not belong to you, and be able to clarify why you feel strongly about parenting in a certain way. Ask long term questions, what do we want for our children? List these values and hopes that you have as parents out and then set about ways of bringing allowing these qualities and strengths to be given space to flourish. It took the two of you to make your children. As you continue nurturing them emotionally and physically you both have important qualities and strengths that you can pass on and the choice to do so now as you parent positively and consciously.
