Putting the theory of Positive Psychology into your daily practice

Martin Seligman or 'Marty' as he calls himself is the founder of Positive Psychology. A highly revered academic who worked in depression for 30 years. The goal of traditional Psychology was to go from very bad to neutral whereas the goal of Positive Psychology is to move past neutral into a state of well-being. It may not seem like a radical idea but it is one that has created an explosion in practical, concept-driven positive psychology theories, from how to be more resilient, to how to create emotional hardiness. Learning how to be optimistic, cultivating and acknowledging your inner strengths. This is the panacea of Positive Psychology rather than an uncomfortable analysis of your weaknesses its offerings are to examine your signature strengths and Values in Action (VIA) and it is under this paradigm the strengths model has been born. I would recommend Penn University's Authentic Happiness website, with a plethora of Positive Psychology questionnaires. The great thing about it is that not only is it free but your results are kept on file allowing you to check back in years later to see if your optimism levels or happiness levels have changed.
      
http://www.authentichappiness.sas.upenn.edu/questionnaires.aspx

Ask any Positive Psychologist what they feel about positive thinking and you may be surprised by their response. Positive thinking in itself is not only not helpful but it can actually make you feel worse. For example if you have anxiety or depression and someone tells you to think more positively you are shutting down a very important aspect of psychological healing that is to experience and process difficult and uncomfortable emotions. The purpose of our emotions are to help us gain insight into how we feel about something. I feel all emotions are somewhat neutral in their importance as they all serve very different purposes. If someone you love dies and you jump straight to thinking positively you do not only do a great disservice to how much that person meant to you but, these repressed feelings will manifest at a later stage.

So, how do you become a happier more content person with a sense of well-being in your life? It's back to the psycho-educational basics with this one, your diet, exercise, sleep hygiene and emotional health are pivotal to the quality of your life. Negative thinking, ruminating and circular reasoning are highly destructive to your well-being. So how do you get to the other 'happier' side? To do this you need to pass the important bridge of consciousness that allows you to experience, feel, process and learn from habitual thought patters and beliefs systems that may be defunct in your current life.

Keeping a diary of your mood triggers can help you on the road with this process. We often brush offenses off without giving much thought to our reactive heuristics or our default thought processes. Even feeling embarrassed that you are as hurt as you feel because you 'know' you shouldn't be. But, by gaining insight into what really bothered you, you may discover that you have more neagtive or pessimistic tendencies.

What are these you may ask, well, lets just take two colleagues both being made redundant the pessimist would be more likely to react to this setbacks from a presumption of personal helplessness. The pessimist might feel that the situation is personal (the boss really hated me) universal (the economy is doomed) and is their fault (I must have been really bad at my job). The optimist on the other hand dealing with the same problem reacts from a position of personal power and sees the situation as temporary (I'll get another job), specific (they let me whole team go) and hopeful (I might change the way I work and chose somewhere closer to home as I don't like my current commute). What is beautiful about this is that you have the choice in how you react, a common misconception is that optimists are the unrealistic ones when it seems through logical deduction of the above example that the pessimist is more likely to get trapped in a negative spiral that would have an impact on their future success.

      
      
      
      
Mihaly Csíkszentmihályi coined the term flow and I think it is very relevant to yoga practitioners and I wanted to point out the positives of your practice. Someone doing yoga may experience the phenomenon of flow because there is a high degree of concentrating and focusing, you have clear goals, feel personally in control, can experience a distorted sense of time and the loss of self-consciousness. There is a balance between ability level and challenge and the activity is neither too easy nor too difficult. It is intrinsically rewarding so there can be a feeling of effortlessness of action resulting in a the feeling of action and awareness merging. The feeling of flow is a wonderful experience so clap on the back to yourself for your participation, discipline and practice.

Here are some helpful rays of light to have at your arsenal. Emotional hardiness and resilience are very useful and hopeful positive psychology concepts to welcome into your daily practice. There will be setbacks in your life. Life is not as within your control as you think, but, and here is the big but, you have a choice in how you react. Tough times believe it or not bring you way outside your comfort zone and will force you to use skills that you may not have even believed that you had.

The truth of the matter is, that I am continuously impressed by the human spirit, it is formidable. So believe in yourself, bring hope, optimism and a flexible and questioning mind to what you do every day. Practice mindfulness, you are already doing it in your yoga practice. I do not profess to have any understanding of yoga but I remember seeing a wonderful poster with a woman in a very difficult looking pose and it said, and this is not a direct quote that life is similar to a difficult yoga pose. The more you encounter difficulty the more you must consciously breathe into it and relax even deeper. This really resonated with me at such a deep level, we are human and we have a magnitude of possibilities and strengths within us but you must practice how you want to be to achieve the physical aspects of yoga. To create a mind experience of well-being and contentedness you must build new neurological pathways to bring you to a new understanding of where you have come from, where you are now and to where you want to go to. Fill your heart, mind and body with daily constructive thought nourishment to be the person you want to be and try and consciously tap into a state of transcendence whereby your emotional strengths reach outside and beyond your negative thought patterns to leave you feeling more connected and in a dynamic state of entrainment.
