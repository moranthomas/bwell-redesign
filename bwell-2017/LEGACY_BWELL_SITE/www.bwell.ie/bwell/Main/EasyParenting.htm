About Allison

Allison Keating is a registered pyschologist of the Psychological Society of Ireland. She has a Masters In Psychology and a Degree in Behavioural Science in Psychology. She is a regular pscyhological media contributor on TV, radio , papers and magazines. Q: My partner and I are having trouble connecting after the birth of our first child. I feel that he’s now pulled away from me, less talkative than before. Is this normal after the birth? Could it be simple a phase that passes?
      
A: Hi, Thanks for your question. This is a really good question and one that if people’s expectations were managed about how a baby will change your relationship it would help soothe anxieties and fears about your relationship. Having a baby is a major transition for a couple, whilst expectant couples can experience a very tight sense of warm connection the reality of your new bundle of joy’s arrival can leave sleep deprived parents bleary eyed and bickering. Here’s a not so fun fact, by your babies first birthday you will have accrued 700 hours of slept debt. 700! This variable alone will strain the very best relationship. Awareness of this fact can really help defuse intense emotions. As you focus on this fact it can stop you internalizing and projecting issues onto your relationship, when the fact is you might both just be exhausted.

Alyson Fearnley Shapiro says that the quality of friendship before the baby arrives is the best predictor of marital adjustment to a new baby. Be kind to each other. Here is one simple but lovely tip. Give each other the benefit of the doubt. This Psychological buffer will provide a marital cushion from the difficulties of irritation, tiredness, hurt feelings and disappointment.

The Journal of Personality and Social Psychology shows that having a baby impacts a marriage in ways new parents never anticipated. Research done by the ‘Bringing Baby Home Program’ shows that relationship satisfaction decreased up to 67 percent for new parents. Ok, fear not, my objective is not a fear mongering exercise but a reality check. It is ok to not be perfect, in fact revel in it. Give your husband a break, and ask him nicely to do likewise! Gottman a world renowned marriage expert, Psychologist and mathematician has worked out the problem for us. There’s a little ratio, that will protect your marriage that you need to put into practice. It’s the 5:1 ratio, so, for every one negative comment you say, you need to say 5 positives. Thanks Gottman, as if you didn’t have enough to do! Change nappy, burp baby, five positives to my husband! Great!

In one respect I feel the transition to motherhood can be less abrupt for a woman as you have been pregnant for nine months. Whereas, for your husband even though he knew you were having a baby he didn’t experience the hormonal, physical or emotional changes. For a couple who are used to it just being the two of ‘us’ men can feel disconnected when ‘us’ goes from two to three people, and you know what they say, three can feel like a crowd. Do you think your husband feels slightly left out?
      
      
      
      
      
      
Don’t despair this catharsis can be one of the most fundamental shifts in your relationship when you go from a couple to a family. Ask your husband how he feels? Listen to his worries, fears and or anger. Resentment at the beginning of a new family can be normal as you take on new responsibilities, roles and ways of living. Spontaneity goes out the window, so this can be a fundamental paradigm shift as you leave behind your old relationship and move towards a new sense of ‘us’.

Communication and lot of it is all you need to get through this. New parents fear how they feel and worry what that means. But when you perhaps allow each other to voice fears and vulnerabilities it can deepen the level of your intimacy and sense of connection to each other.

A great sense of humour is another beautiful tool in your new parenting toolkit. Be specific with your husband about what you would like help with. He can help with everything except the breastfeeding! But don’t expect him to read your mind, ask him, and be mindful of your tone. If you don’t feel ready to be physically intimate, and neither of you might, do give cuddles, connect with hugs, reassurance and warmth.
