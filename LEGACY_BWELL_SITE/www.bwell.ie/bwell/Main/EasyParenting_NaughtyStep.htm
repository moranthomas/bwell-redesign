The Naughty Step
      
      
Allison says: Ok, so here it is, I don't believe in the naughty step. When discussing that I was going to do an interview on TV3's The Morning Show about this I knew I was going to face some major parental opposition. Sometimes the issues I feel I have to defend myself against are usually the least scientifically backed and yet they are certainly often the most popular. So, let's put the naughty step on trial. Firstly, just because it's popular doesn't make it effective or right. I am always interested in seeing how concepts specifically relating to parenting go viral. Just because Supernanny says so, doesn't mean it works. Many a dutiful and diligent parent has used this in their parenting toolbox without perhaps questioning it's validity. I am a scientist at heart, I like cause and effect, but more importantly I like evidence based techniques.
      
      
Unfortunately many experts out there are vying for shelf-space that their method works best, some of it is anecdotal, some of it may work some of the time but the main problem is that it won't work all of the time. The main issue is that good techniques given in the wrong way will lead to a parent abandoning the method because it didn't work for them. This is when I invite the vigour's of testing and empiricism to have a seat.

My first expert witness on the ineffectiveness of the naughty step is Dr. Alan Kazdin, head of the Yale Parenting Center & Child Conduct Clinic with 30 years of experience with over 6000 children and their families. As a Psychologist in terms of change happening I recognise the importance of 'what to do and how to do it' weighs in far more than the 'why it's happening'. Kazdin also advocates this. Parents think that the 'timeout or naughty step' will give the child time to reflect and understand what they have done. But, this is the first problem of why the naughty step doesn't work, the child doesn't learn a lesson because you need to tell the child what you do want them to do. This is what Kazdin calls the positive opposite. Your brain intellectually understands negatives but in terms of giving a command you have to tell your brain what you want it to do in the positive. e.g. 'Stop hitting your sister'. You need to tell your child the positive opposite of this which is 'play nicely with your sister' and when they do, you praise them and focus on the good behaviour. The focus needs a complete reversal as whatever you give attention to, will grow.

      
      
      
      
      
Here's the vicious circle, you give a time out, for bad behaviour but you haven't changed the original negative behaviour so it will happen again and when it does you might up the time ante and the problem will become worse.

Excessive timeouts can do more harm than you even realise, they can harm the child's self-esteem and the child can begin to avoid and escape from the adult who is admonishing the punishment.


'Timeout from reinforcement' is the original name of time outs as we know them. Basically the idea is just to give a break from attention, any attention, so as not to feed the unwanted behaviour.

Here is the Kazdin method of how to use a timeout correctly:

1. Use it sparingly
2. Make it Brief
3. Do it immediately
4. Do it in isolation from others
5. Administered calmly

The most important aspect the research concluded with for the effectiveness of timeouts was the effectiveness of 'time in'. Follow the ABC method, of antecedent, behaviour and consequences:

1. Antecedent - What is the problem – Pinching your sister

2. Behaviour - What behaviour do you want? - Being kind and gentle with said sister!

3. Consequences – Reinforce the good behaviour – 'Well done Lucy, you are being very gentle with your little sister' said in an upbeat way with a reinforcing touch such as a high five or a hug.

Timeouts and naughty steps/chairs won't change the negative behaviour, try the above steps and let me know how you get on.

Remember the importance of self-care for yourself. I would recommend self-imposed time outs for parents as even just a quick physical break with some nice deep breaths can help you feel calm, bring your heart rate down and allow you to return with a clearer head. It is easy to become angry and frustrated and to feel like your losing control. But, once you lose control the child gains control.

I am looking forward to answering your questions and I hope to focus on parental mental health, as a happy mum and dad are happy kids. Being a good parent is a full-time job, and it is so important to look after yourself because every time you fly you hear the best parenting advice out there. They tell you to put on your oxygen mask first, that to be able to look after your children it is not an option but a pre-requisite to mind yourself first.

Looking forward to answering your questions, Allison x
