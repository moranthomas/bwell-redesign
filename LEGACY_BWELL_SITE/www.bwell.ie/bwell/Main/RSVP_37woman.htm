About Allison

Allison Keating is a registered pyschologist of the Psychological Society of Ireland. She has a Masters In Psychology and a Degree in Behavioural Science in Psychology. She is a regular pscyhological media contributor on TV, radio , papers and magazines. Q: I am 37 years old, married for seven years and we have three children. I had always been happily married until things started to change in our relationship on our last two children. I was trying to juggle a very busy and demanding job, with three small children and someone along the way I feel myself and my husband have lost ‘us’. I think my husband sees me now as a wife and mother rather than his lover and our intimacy has changed dramatically. He still wants to have sex but I don’t feel I can as I don’t feel like we seem like strangers. I think I have developed a crush about a guy in my office, he is really attentive to me, compliments me and seems to be interested in me as a person. I find myself opening up to him more and more and I am scared where this is going. Please help. J x
      
      
Hi J, thank you for your e-mail, I’m sorry to hear that you feel conflicted and lonely in your marriage. A number of issues seem to be of concern for you. Firstly, there is a sense of disappointment about the relationship in your marriage. From what you have said it sounds like you feel alone in the relationship and not appreciated as a woman who can be the mother, wife, friend and lover.

There are a lot of roles being played out here, and to keep the intimate flame alive you really have to work at it in a marriage. Sometimes having children does change a man’s perspective of who you are, and some men can struggle over the wholesome idea of a being a mother with being odds with being sexy and passionate with their wife.

For you, it sounds like you miss the physically intimate part of your relationship but that you don’t feel emotionally connected enough to feel safe to go there. Women and men connect to sex differently, men feel connected after sex and women need to feel connected before they have sex, go figure that they are at odds with each other. But, it is a good balance between the physical and emotional. Do you want to stay committed and faithful to your husband? Apart from the fantasies you seem to be having about this affair with the man in your office have you really played the consequences of a potential affair to completion? How would it affect your marriage, your children, yourself?

There seems to be an element of escapism with the office crush. It sounds like your getting your emotional needs met by the nice compliments from the man at work, but does he really know you? You are in dangerous territory because physical affairs usually are born from emotional affairs, by being intimate about your feelings. What would it be like to share these thoughts with your husband? About feeling lonely, unappreciated, undesired? Could you talk to him about how he feels about you since having a family. Has it changed his perception of you as the sexy woman he knew to being ‘the mom’. You don’t know until you ask. When was the last time you spoke to your husband about your fears, concerns or worries of an intimate nature. What I mean by the word intimate isn’t purely sexual it is in the context of being emotionally intimate, which leads to feelings of being connected to each other, in love and cared for.
      
      
      
      
      

Affairs are destructive, I know a French Psychologist said in her book ‘they can spice things up, but this is Ireland and I think once the trust is gone you are in for one major struggle to regain the sacred union of trust between two people. You are looking for love, companionship, affection and you are right, you deserve these things. I gently suggest that if you love your husband, that you reflect upon the feelings that you are having and instead of looking outside of yyour marriage that you turn back towards your marriage and work together possibly with the help of a couples counsellor or in your own therapy to express your desires, hopes and future relational expectations and to work towards them. You say that you feel that you have lost the ‘us’ in your relationship, ask yourself what made your relationship special and what qualities and feelings made up that unique sense of the ‘us’ in your marriage. I would also suggest doing the same for yourself, as your identity and what makes you ‘you’ may need to be recaptured. We are constantly changing give yourself the space to figure out what would help you and this situation and make a new shared meaning for you, your relationship and your family that integrates all your strengths and roles as a woman. Good luck on your personal journey and I wish you all the best.
