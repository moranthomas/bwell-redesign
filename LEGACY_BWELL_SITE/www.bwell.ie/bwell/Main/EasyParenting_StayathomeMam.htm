About Allison

Allison Keating is a registered pyschologist of the Psychological Society of Ireland. She has a Masters In Psychology and a Degree in Behavioural Science in Psychology. She is a regular pscyhological media contributor on TV, radio , papers and magazines. The bWell Clinic is a progressive, emphatic psychology clinic that uses CBT - solution focused therapy, pscyo-dynamic therapy and positive psychology for pscyhological well being specialising in anxiety, panic attack, relationships and advocating good mental health. Click here to follow the bWell Clinic on facebook for relevent and interesting topics on all things to do with your mind and emotions.
      
Q: For financial reasons, my partner and I decided that I would be a stay at home mother. We have 15 month old twin boys and a four year old girl. I am finding life tough and really over whelming. It's hard work looking after the kids and staying on top of household stuff. Also, I feel as if my family and friends don't really understand how hard it is for me and that they think I sit watching TV most of the day drinking tea. I wish that was the case! I have actually never felt so alone and I'm finding it difficult to explain how lonely and sad I feel to my husband. I don't want to feel like this - how can I become more positive about my situation?
      
      
      
A: I love this depiction of a stay at home mom because I think it really hits the nail on the head. Fair play to you, you are a busy lady, twin boys who are 15 months and a little four year old girl, sure you must have so much time for that cup of tea and couch surfing! You have three children under the age of five, double trouble on the twins front! No matter how good they are they are wholly emotionally and physically dependent on you and this is hugely draining and tiring.

Here is one eye-achingly helpful solution to help your husband understand. Ask him to mind the children on his own for one full week, and just wait as he’ll be begging you to come back. On a serious note you sound genuinely lonely and isolated. I would suggest going to group activities, these don’t all have a cost as a lot of libraries and communities have mum and toddler groups. You need to be connecting with other mothers, in a similar boat to yours. You need another kind empathic ear who lives in similar shoes, to say, ‘I understand’. I think it is so important for adults to stay connected, no matter how much people give out about their work colleagues they still provide some ‘entertainment’ or fodder for conversation. One of the downsides of being a stay at home mom is the loss of peer socializing.

How long have you felt like this? Do you ever feel depressed and or anxious? If you do, contact your GP or a mental health practitioner to discuss how you are feeling. It was the word ‘overwhelmed’ that caught my ear. Overwhelmed to my ears raises a flag about feeling it is all too much. The Tsunami of feelings that it’s too much for you to cope with. One huge part of this could be that you may be exhausted physically and emotionally. Children are genuinely such a blessing, and really provide so much joy. But, they are also incredibly, relentlessly demanding of you time and energy. You need to be very careful when you are a full-time stay at home mum that you have scheduled built in time for yourself. You have to create boundaries, because it is in your nature as a mother to give and give until often the colds keep coming and you get really run down. I would suggest exercise as your first port of call, it really changes your emotional state. I am a big fan of exercise classes, as it has a built in socializing factor even if it is in universal moaning at bootcamp or salsa instructor!

Ask yourself this question. ‘Who are you?’. A lot of mothers lose so much confidence in themselves when their children are young. This can be multi-factorial, if you had a job that you identified yourself with before, some women can feel somewhat flat saying ‘I’m just a mum’ etc, I hear this a lot. Deep down they know it is the hardest job in the world, but on the outside do we revere the mother to the status that she deserves? I don’t think so, unfortunately we give status to paid jobs, and mothers should be paid as I think it would change the mothers role and status at a societal value level.

When the mommy wars fight over ‘ambition or attachment’ I feel women are at odds with themselves and are not sharing how difficult it can be. Women are known for being good listeners and empathic but I feel that warmth has gone between women and there is a lot of oppositional 'them' and 'us'. This needs to be dropped, women need to come together to laugh at your own mistakes and foibles that you do with yourself and your children. Women are competitive, but I feel if we were more accepting and that everyone is doing their best and to really listen and be there for each other the sense of being judged would radically decrease and bring with it a sense of being connected and a bond of understanding would deepen to what it’s like being a modern mom.

      
      
      
      
      
In answering your question ‘Who am I now?’ don’t be frightened if you don’t know the answer anymore, this is usually the cue for lots of pent up tears to come flooding out. Let them out, pregnancy is a huge emotional roller-coaster and then motherhood keeps that ride just keep on going.

You need to connect with your husband over dinner (go out) and explain how you really feel, be vulnerable, ask him to hear you before he tries to fix you. The male brain gets jittery when he sees his wife get upset and jumps into caveman ‘I can fix it mode’ very quickly as it makes his brain feel uncomfortable to see you upset. This is where you need to let your husband into all the fears, worries, vulnerabilities and sheer exhaustion you are feeling. From not knowing who you are anymore, to expressing to him how thankless housecleaning is. I genuinely think housecleaning is emotionally demoralizing. Let me develop that point…I’m trying to visualize your house and I can imagine that you have put everything away haven’t sat down once all morning and then before you know it you are into the lunchtime carnage and in about 30 seconds, maybe even twenty, toys are flung everywhere as you try and not kill yourself from flying up in the air to carry the freshly ironed clothes upstairs with miniscule yet highly menacing blocks and the like underfoot doing their best to get you on your back.

Figure out which friends you need to understand you and where you feel you are at at present. Explain to them how you feel, to people who are not as involved in your daily life you don’t have to prove how hard it is to them, accepting it yourself is enough. I hear from your words that you need to feel understood, you need to connect and get a sense of validation over how you are feeling at the moment. So, start with your husband, it might help to have a writing rant before you do this, sit down and write out all the things that are getting under your skin at the moment. Note the things that you feel you ‘shouldn’t’ be upset about. Again, my ears always prick up when a client says ‘this is really silly, or stupid’. This means they feel they should be able to deal with it but they can’t and then they further chastise themselves for feeling overwhelmed about a silly thing in the first place. The problem is that if you don’t listen to the real irritation or upset you miss out on the opportunity to understand what the real feeling is, for example. You may get really angry over your husband leaving his cup in the living room, as you have asked him again and again to put it away. You get really angry and then this mood ruminates and builds for the day, before you know it, you feel that he mustn’t care about you at all and you really haven’t been getting on for months so your marriage must be in real trouble! Now, do you see why it’s so important to write this out before you talk to your husband. It is the combination of minding the children and all that entails, cooking, cleaning, and then doing it all again, so this can feel so endless that you can feel very negative and stuck.

Begin by going out and buying yourself a nice little jotter or diary (Penney's have lovely ones for €1.50). This is your gratitude diary, and each night just before bed write out three positive things that happened that day and or three things you are grateful for. Nothing else goes in this diary. This is a very powerful exercise and so worthwhile.

Secondly, write a schedule out and have it on the wall, you need to have times where you get to go out on your own. Also put in times where you do and or go fun places with the kids and as a family and thirdly go on dates with your husband.

I love September time, this is like my New Year, so check out a course of something that will bring you joy, and do it. All these things are behavioural actions, nothing will change if you just think about it. To help you make behavioural changes I have a 15 day DSD (Do Something Different) plan based on Professor Flethchers work on the bWell Clinic facebook page I would suggest doing this. By doing something different everyday it creates real behavioural changes that open the door to making bigger changes.

Having connected again with your husband, mother and friends, engaged in consistent exercise, your gratitude diary and your 15 DSD plan you will see the changes. If you would still like help then take the step to work through this with a mental health practitioner. Be kind, empathic and loving to yourself, start by having some compassion for yourself. Be mindful of how you speak to yourself and if you are being harsh or judgmental ask yourself if you would speak to a friend like this. The put that friendly tone back into your own daily internal chatter and this will really help you connect back to yourself again,

Best of luck, Allison
