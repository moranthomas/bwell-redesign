The rollercoaster of pregnancy emotions

We often associate emotional outbursts with atypical pregnant woman stereotypes. The irate pregnant lady looking to be feed, or brought some tea, like, now (and don't forget the chocolate)! Or tearfully tucking into the next Kleenex to a soppy rom-com, but that was just me when I was pregnant. Pregnancy in all it's glory is amazing, but that mother nature and those hormones can leave some tumultuous highs and lows in it's blazing pregnancy trail. I wanted to give you a quick visual above to show you how your hormones play such a major role in your emotional state during pregnancy. I, for one, as a mother of two, can say with experience that I have been quite taken aback (and I'm sure my husband was as well!) from being my ‘normal’ self to a quite sudden and unprovoked Jekyll and Hyde mood switch (ok, let's be honest, mood switches, plural definitely plural) during pregnancy. I remember crying deeply into my pasta bowl over dinner one night and then sheepishly laughing because I really had no idea why I was crying! This admission comes with a caveat and warning to all men and partners out there. Your pregnant wife or partner does not want to hear the words ‘it’s just your hormones’ utter from your lips, or prepare to meet some little pregnancy hormone friends of mine called progesterone, estrogen and Human chorionic gonadotrophin who lost their sense of humour, like, yesterday.

From conception to birth there is a lot of one very special element, time. In life we are used to having everything faster and quicker. But being pregnant requires you and your developing miracle to be in it for the long haul, and it’s ten months, people. For some even the initial understanding that you are pregnant, from a bewildered and shocked head down the toilet wondering 'what is wrong with me?', to one of joy to one of complete fear can be attributed to our aforementioned friend hCG. A well known book called ‘What to expect when you are expecting’ is a very valuable reference to all mothers to get you from week one to forty but, I think I might write another version called ‘Expect the unexpected’. In my version I think I would stress to pregnant ladies to consider what you and your body is doing. I have to confess I was an avid reader of the week by week pregnancy e-mails during pregnancy and I was utterly fascinated with the hive of activity within. So I think a warm dose of compassion is needed to allow yourself deal with the mood swings that come around quickly on the pregnancy roundabout.

This time waiting is quite disconcerting for the modern woman. Modern women usually enjoy a sense of personal autonomy and feel a sense of control. I think pregnancy can challenge this, and hormones rustle up some serious emotional contenders to play ball with. You may have heard of the infamous 'Day 3', this is three days after giving birth and is known to have many a woman who felt on top of the world yesterday, a female warrior who gave birth to a human being, to waking up on day three feeling very vulnerable, sore, and emotionally overwhelmed. Accompanied in no small measure by some serious tears. Well, you can thank a 70% drop in progesterone for this one alone. This is a phenomenal hormonal change.

Feelings of insecurity and vulnerability can rear their emotional heads during pregnancy. Talk with your partner and to family and friends who will have a kind ear and who will listen and acknowledge your fears. For some women they embrace there new curves, for others it can be greeted with hostility. Acceptance is a term in psychology that has its place amid the pregnancy state. If you go with, rather than against how you are right now and stay quite mindful that whatever stage of pregnancy you are at, good or bad , that it will pass. How you perceive how you feel will impact upon how you will subsequently feel.

Stress can also rise in pregnancy, as you are in a state of flux and if you are a first time mom you may have fears of the unknown and for the mother with children she may have fears of the known. One thing you can control during pregnancy is your reaction to these feelings of stress, fears or anxieties. A useful CBT (cognitive behavioural technique) is to 'Stop, Think and Breathe'. This technique does what it says on the tin. You decide to become aware and stop any negative thinking and then think about how that negative thought is impacting you. Then breathe all the way into your abdomen.
      
      
      
      
It can be nice to do this with your hands on your stomach as it is quite satisfying to see a lovely pregnant belly rise and fall. Breathing is a wonderful antidote to stress. Shallow breathing sends a signal to your brain to feel tense and release stress hormones. All you need to do is to take a nice deep breath in for four, hold for four and release for four, or breathe whatever way feels most comfortable for you. This technique could be a nice way to decide that you are purposefully relaxing for you and your baby.

Write out your fear and anxieties. Putting them on paper helps your mind see them in linear order. Worry prefers to go around and around, making it difficult to make a decision and you can feel stuck by the enormity of all the tasks you have set yourself. Sit down with the Psychologists number one weapon, a pen, and, wait for it, a piece of paper. Either do this on your own or with your partner, list your fears, and then discuss them. Even saying them out loud helps disempower even gripping fears. The power of writing down your concerns means you have taken them off the heavy mantle of your head and now you can do something about it. Professor Fletcher who created FIT science (framework for internal transformation) would advocate his DSD model, Do Something Different. With each fear and concern, come up with the worst case scenario first, for the partner do not dismiss this but acknowledge it. Then discuss is this feeling completely true, my test I ask with clients is 'would it stand up in court?' In other words is it a fact or is it your opinion? Then come up with alternative ways to think about your fear stated in the positive. If the feeling is still present use a highly effective CBT technique called 'Soften Soothe and Allow'. Whereby, you feel the emotion in your body and soften it, soothe the feeling and as you do acknowledge how you feel and this allows you to release any feelings that may need to surface and be released.

So if you have a fear of labour perhaps you could use this model. Firstly, write out your fear. There is a lot of cultural and media propaganda about labour. Sentences such as 'they don't call it labour for nothing' and every woman from strangers to mothers telling you of their horrific birth story do not help prepare women for birth. Instead of the atypical TV image of the screaming, swearing, biting woman in birth, do yourself a favour and have a look at some cultures who view birth as an important and wonderful spiritual experience. Pain and fear go hand in hand. Your body has a whole host of amazingly designed hormones to help with your birth and fear is the nemesis of this. I am not saying birth will be pain free but Western culture breeds fear with labour and this is the one emotion that won't help you in the labour ward. So mentally prepare yourself to have a good birth experience, which again can be difficult because in so much as you are in control you are equally not in control and this is where acceptance needs to come back into the room.

If the labour scaremongering starts, firmly (but with a smile on your face) say you are apprehensive about the experience so you'd love to hear their story after your baby is born. In terms of Doing Something Different I would prepare yourself for the birth experience. I felt very passionately about this so I made a bWell pre-labour relaxation Mp3 (bwell.ie) to mentally and physically prepare for giving birth. It is a good idea to filter what you read and think about in terms of labour. A conscious decision to understand how to make your hormones work for you during birth is more important than the million other nesting induced lists and tasks you set yourself.

The transition of becoming a mother can bring up a whole host of psychological feelings about what type of mother you will be and this can also trigger feelings and sometimes unresolved issues with your own mother. If you feel depressed about this it might be worth talking with a therapist. It can be normal to have stronger feelings but if there are a lot of past issues it can be a good idea to resolve these before the baby arrives. On a positive note, after you have your baby you will have a new found respect and a deeper understanding for what it must have been like for your mother.

When you are handed your newborn baby there is no other feeling like it. The room at this point is a heady cocktail of the love hormone oxytocin, prolactin and a beautiful scrunched up little face staring wide-eyed up at you. This is when you know nothing else matters and you truly understand the enormity of the task you have just achieved and made from conception to birth (your partner helped a bit!) over the course of your 40 weeks (give or take a few) and you know from the bottom of your soul every hormone and emotion was worth it.
